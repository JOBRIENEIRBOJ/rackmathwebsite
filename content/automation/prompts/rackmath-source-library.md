# RackMath Source Library

## Purpose

This file gives the RackMath Blogger GPT reliable sources to use when writing articles about beginner resistance training, health, strength, form, consistency, and workout habits.

The generator should use sources carefully.

Do not invent citations.

Do not cite a source unless it actually supports the claim being made.

## Citation Rules

Use citations for claims about:

- Physical activity guidelines
- Strength training recommendations
- Muscle health
- Bone health
- Aging
- Injury risk
- Training frequency
- Health outcomes
- Research findings
- Medical or scientific statements

Citations are usually not needed for common practical advice like:

- "Start with a lighter weight."
- "Write down your sets."
- "Ask gym staff if you do not know how to adjust equipment."

But if the article says something like:

> Adults should do muscle-strengthening activity at least two days per week.

That needs a citation.

## Preferred Sources

### 1. CDC Physical Activity Guidelines for Adults

Use for:

- Adults need at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity weekly.
- Adults should do muscle-strengthening activity at least 2 days per week.
- Some activity is better than none.
- Physical activity supports long-term health.

Source:
https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html

### 2. WHO Physical Activity Recommendations

Use for:

- Adults should do muscle-strengthening activities involving major muscle groups on 2 or more days per week.
- Physical activity supports health across age groups.
- Global physical activity recommendations.

Source:
https://www.who.int/initiatives/behealthy/physical-activity

### 3. ACSM Physical Activity Guidelines

Use for:

- Resistance training as part of a balanced activity plan.
- General exercise recommendations.
- Strength training frequency.
- Physical activity guidance from a major professional organization.

Source:
https://acsm.org/education-resources/trending-topics-resources/physical-activity-guidelines/

### 4. ACSM Resistance Training Guidance

Use for:

- Resistance training principles.
- Strength training consistency.
- Beginner-friendly resistance training.
- Resistance training does not have to be overly complex.
- Benefits can come from multiple types of resistance, including free weights, machines, bands, and bodyweight.

Source:
https://acsm.org/resistance-training-guidelines-update-2026/

### 5. NIH / National Institute on Aging

Use for:

- Strength training and aging.
- Maintaining strength for daily life.
- Older adults and exercise.
- Balance, independence, and function.

Source:
https://www.nia.nih.gov/health/exercise-and-physical-activity

### 6. Mayo Clinic Strength Training

Use for:

- General benefits of strength training.
- Muscle mass, bone strength, weight management, and daily function.
- Simple patient-friendly explanations.

Source:
https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/fitness/in-depth/strength-training/art-20046670

### 7. Cleveland Clinic Strength Training

Use for:

- Plain-language explanation of strength training benefits.
- Beginner-friendly health information.
- General exercise education.

Source:
https://health.clevelandclinic.org/strength-training

### 8. PubMed / Research Studies

Use for:

- Specific research claims.
- More detailed evidence.
- Claims about resistance training outcomes.

Source:
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/

Only use PubMed studies when the claim is specific enough to need research support.

## Safe Claim Examples

Good:

> The CDC recommends adults include muscle-strengthening activity at least two days per week.

Good:

> Strength training can help maintain muscle and support daily function as people age.

Good:

> Beginners do not need a complicated plan to start. They need a simple plan they can repeat.

Risky:

> Strength training prevents disease.

Better:

> Strength training is one part of a healthy physical activity plan and is recommended by major health organizations.

Risky:

> Lifting weights guarantees you will avoid injury.

Better:

> Learning good technique and using weights you can control can help make training more manageable for beginners.

## Citation Format for Blog Posts

Use this format at the end of Markdown articles:

```md
## Sources

- CDC. "Adult Activity: An Overview." https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html
- WHO. "Physical Activity." https://www.who.int/initiatives/behealthy/physical-activity
- American College of Sports Medicine. "Physical Activity Guidelines." https://acsm.org/education-resources/trending-topics-resources/physical-activity-guidelines/
```

## Citation Checker Rules

Before publishing, check:

1. Does every health or training claim need a source?
2. Does the source actually support the claim?
3. Is the claim overstated?
4. Can the claim be rewritten more safely?
5. Are the sources reputable?

When in doubt, simplify the claim.
