## Section Guidance

### Opening Hook

The opening should make the reader feel understood.

Good opening types:

- A relatable beginner gym moment
- A simple problem
- A common fear
- A direct question
- A short story

Example:

> Your first week of lifting does not need to be impressive. It needs to be repeatable.

Example:

> The first time you load a barbell, it can feel like a math test with metal circles.

### The Simple Truth

State the main idea clearly.

Example:

> The best beginner workout is not the perfect workout. It is the one you can repeat without getting hurt, confused, or so sore you never want to come back.

### Why This Matters

Explain why the reader should care.

Use this section for health, confidence, consistency, or practical importance.

Cite sources here if making health or training claims.

### What Beginners Usually Get Wrong

Name the common mistake without shaming the reader.

Examples:

- Starting too heavy
- Changing the workout every session
- Not tracking anything
- Worrying too much about what others think
- Ignoring form
- Doing too many exercises
- Getting lost in plate math

### What To Do Instead

Give practical steps.

Use numbered lists when helpful.

Example:

```md
1. Pick 3–5 basic exercises.
2. Start with a weight you can control.
3. Leave a few reps in the tank.
4. Write down what you did.
5. Add weight slowly when the reps look clean.
```

### How RackMath Helps

Mention RackMath naturally.

Good:

> RackMath helps take one small headache out of the workout: figuring out what plates go on the bar. That matters more than people think when you are new and already trying to remember your form, your reps, and where your water bottle went.

Bad:

> RackMath is the ultimate tool for transforming your fitness journey.

### Final Thought

End short.

Do not turn the final paragraph into a motivational poster.

Good:

> Start light. Track your work. Come back next week. That is how this gets built.
