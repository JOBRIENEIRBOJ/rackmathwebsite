# RackMath Writing Style Guide

## Core Voice

RackMath should sound like a normal person helping another normal person get comfortable with lifting weights.

The voice should be:

- Plainspoken
- Real
- Practical
- Slightly vulnerable when appropriate
- Helpful without sounding like a fitness influencer
- Calm, clear, and direct
- Occasionally funny, but never forced
- Encouraging without hype

The reader should feel:

> "Okay, I can actually do this."

## What RackMath Should Not Sound Like

Avoid:

- Fitness bro language
- Overhyped motivational talk
- Long academic explanations
- Corporate wellness language
- Vague inspirational fluff
- Excessive adjectives
- Complicated jargon without explanation
- Shaming the reader for being new
- Making lifting sound harder than it needs to be

Avoid phrases like:

- "Crush your goals"
- "Unlock your full potential"
- "Dominate the gym"
- "Transform your body fast"
- "No excuses"
- "Beast mode"
- "Revolutionize your fitness journey"

## Reading Level

Write so a 12-year-old can understand it.

That means:

- Short sentences
- Simple words
- Clear examples
- Short paragraphs
- One idea at a time
- Explain gym terms when needed

But do not talk down to adults. The reader may be smart, busy, tired, nervous, or embarrassed. Respect that.

## Sentence Style

Prefer:

> Start light. Learn the movement. Add weight when your form still looks solid.

Avoid:

> Beginners should prioritize progressive overload while simultaneously optimizing neuromuscular adaptation and movement efficiency.

## Paragraph Style

Use short paragraphs.

Most paragraphs should be 1–3 sentences.

White space is good. The reader may be on a phone, standing in a gym parking lot, wondering if they should even walk in.

## Humor Style

Use light humor from real beginner awkwardness.

Good example:

> The first time you adjust a squat rack, you may look like you are trying to open a bank vault. That is normal.

Bad example:

> Only weak people are scared of the gym.

Humor should make the reader feel less alone, not embarrassed.

## Emotional Tone

The emotional tone should be:

- "I remember what it felt like to start."
- "You do not need to be perfect."
- "Here is the next simple step."
- "Do the basics. Track your work. Keep going."

## Writing Priorities

Every article should prioritize:

1. Clarity
2. Usefulness
3. Honesty
4. Simplicity
5. Reader confidence

## Editing Rules

When editing a RackMath article:

- Cut filler.
- Remove repeated points.
- Replace vague advice with specific action.
- Keep the tone human.
- Keep paragraphs short.
- Remove unnecessary adjectives.
- Remove hype.
- Make the article easier to read.
- Make sure every section earns its place.

## Preferred Style

Use language like:

- "Here is the simple version."
- "Start with a weight you can control."
- "You are not behind. You are learning."
- "The goal is not to look smooth. The goal is to show up and practice."
- "Do not make the plan so complicated that you never start."
- "Small progress still counts."
- "Leave a little in the tank."
- "The less you have to think about plate math, the more you can focus on the lift."

## RackMath Brand Position

RackMath is not trying to be the loudest fitness brand.

RackMath is the helpful tool in your pocket that makes lifting less confusing.

RackMath helps with:

- Barbell plate math
- Weight lifting calculations
- Workout tracking
- Progress history
- Reducing friction in the gym
- Helping beginners feel less lost

## Natural RackMath Mentions

Good:

> When you are new, plate math is one more thing to think about. RackMath helps you load the bar correctly so you can focus on the lift.

Bad:

> RackMath is the ultimate revolutionary platform that will change your fitness life forever.

## Final Test

Before publishing, ask:

- Would a beginner understand this?
- Did we make lifting feel more doable?
- Did we give useful steps?
- Did we cut the fluff?
- Does it sound like a real person wrote it?
